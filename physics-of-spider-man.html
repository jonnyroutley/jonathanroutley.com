SECTION 1

THE PHYSICS OF SPIDER-MAN

Spider-Man first appeared over 55 years ago, in the fifteenth installment of the comic book Amazing Fantasy. Since its inception in August 1962, Spider-Man has gone on to be one of the most popular and commercially successful superheroes ever. Many attribute this to the fact that Spider-Man, or Peter Parker, was a new breed of superhero who was relatable in a way that the traditional comic book titans such as Batman and Superman weren't[1]. He's a not-so-cool teenager who gets picked on by bullies, isn't good with girls and has to face everyday struggles like the rest of us. It's only when Peter is bitten by a radioactive spider on a school trip to a lab that he develops powers: namely super strength, the ability to scale walls effortlessly and 'spider senses'. This section looks at some of the physics of Spider-Man, primarily using the 2012 film (starring Andrew Garfield) The Amazing Spider-Man as the main reference point. Although a new incarnation of Spider-Man has recently appeared in the Marvel film universe (with him getting his own film released earlier this Summer), I chose to use The Amazing Spider-Man as my main reference point due to it being the most recently made film available to purchase and watch at home/replay through it, thus making my research process easier. I also love that the director of the film is aptly called Marc Webb. The different questions I look at are nested within a dropdown menu system below:

How Strong are Spider-Man's Webs?

While some adaptations of Spider-Man show the webs that the eponymous hero famously employs as an organic subtance that he produces within himself, most versions (this film included) portray the webs as an externally manufactured substance. In this particular film, the web has been originally created by the giant trans-national corporation 'Oscorp', packed into small canisters and then distributed accordingly. These canisters are then remodelled by Peter Parker to fit into a small wrist-watch sized device which can be triggered via touch sensors on his palms to shoot the web. Is It Faster For Spider-Man To Run Or Swing?

Aside from catching heavy object, Spider-Man also uses his trademark webs as a handy transport system to get around town or to make a quick getaway. Have a look at this scene here which shows Spider-Man making an escape from the police (who are not yet on his side)[c]

The question that this then prompts is this: is it really faster for Spider-Man to swing? If we model his movement like that of a pendulum then we can make it a harmonic motion question. This would mean that the main thing affecting his swing speed is the length of the web he's swinging on - we can see from the video that the arches of the tunnel he attaches his webs to are not that high, and also he takes a non-direct/slower zig-zag route. Would it then potentially be faster for him to skateboard (his skills shown below)[d] or even run?

Especially with the high-rise nature of Manhattan and the great density of skyscrapers, the fact that his velocity increase with pendulum length means that swinging is definitely a good mode of transport at other times (especially to avoid rush-hour traffic).

How Does Spider-Man Stick To Walls?

The final thing we will consider in this section is how Spider-Man sticks to walls. This aspect of his abilities (like many others) has seen several different interpretations in Hollywood. The 2002 film Spider-Man starring Tobey Maguire explains Spider-Man's ability to cling to walls via sticky, retractable hairs on his hands as so[h]:

However, in the film we're referencing, it is seen as an innate ability that Peter Parker wakes up with having been bitten by the radio-active spider. How might we try and explain this? In order to try and find out I consulted the book The Gecko’s Foot: How Scientists are Taking a Leaf from Nature's Book by Peter Forbes[12], which has become a major source for this project. In this book, he talks about the underlying mechanisms that enable geckos (and spiders among other insects/creatures) to seemingly effortlessly traipse unaided up walls and across ceilings. He writes (p.82) with reference to the Tokay Gecko (Gecko gecko):

"The electron microscope shows that there are very many bristles on the toes of a gecko: almost 500,000 on each foot. And more than that, the gecko has, as Autumn says, 'a bad case of split ends': the ends of the bristles fork into between 100 and 1,000 mini-bristles with enlarged and flattened spoon-like endings, spatulas, which, as Bob Full puts it, 'look like broccoli on the is of the hairs'. It is these spatulas that make contact with the surface and a single gecko has about one billion of these points of contact [below [i]]."

The reason that this is so important is that by having bristles on the nano-scale, it means that the gecko can stick to any surface by means of van der Waals forces - not gravitational, electromagnetic or chemical but a force in its own category of intermolecular. The van der Waals force is a type of 'background attraction' that takes place between atoms or molecules and is only effective within a range of about 2nm.

The book also states (p.83-4):

"Autumn's team showed that a single bristle did exhibit the force required to account for the adhesion of the foot: more than that, a single gecko bristle had 10 times more force than you would have expected given the force exerterd by the whole animal and the number of bristles. This suggested that at any one time only a fraction of the bristles was in contact. It also meant that if a gecko did have all its bristles in contact at the same time, it would be able to support a 120-kg man. In the real world it is unusual for two surfaces to touch at many points - surface may look to us as if they are in intimate contact but at the nanolevel hardly anything touches at all. To work effectively, the gecko has a huge margin of error. That is why it moves around so confidently and can suspend itself from a single toe: it knows it has plenty of adhesion in reserve."

So how can we manipulate this knowledge and data in order to apply it to Spider-Man? Although we can probably assume that Spider-Man's mass is less than 120kg, we would want to figure out how many bristles he would need in order to have the same amount of headroom of adhesive power compared to the gecko. Combining the two quotations, we can first say that a typical gecko has 500,000 bristles on each foot, or 2,000,000 bristles in total. These 2,000,000 bristles, if all used would support up to 120kg, or about 1,180N (120 x 9.81 to 3sf). Therefore, each bristle would support 1,180/2,000,000N = 5.9x10-4N (about 0.06 grams).

The average (median) mass of a male Tokay Gecko is 0.234kg[13], which means that it overcompensates the maximum amount of weight it would hold (assuming it just holds its bodyweight) by a factor of around 500 (120/0.234 = 513 (3sf). Assuming Spider-Man is roughly 75kg, this would mean he would be able to hold (as a theoretical maximum) a grand total of 37,500kg. This equates to 63,600,000 (3sf) bristles in total. Although this may sound like a lot, when you consider that an average (median) male gecko is about 30cm long[11] and think of the comparative surface areas covered by the gecko's hands/feet compared to that of a (nearly) adult male, it seems to be more plausible - the density of bristles shouldn't be drastically different.

One final thing that we might want to think about when considering this is how much control Spider-Man would have over his ability to stick to things. In the film, when he first discovers his powers, he involuntarily sticks to objects but slowly over time seems to gain more control over it - it finally appears that it is almost controlled at will. The solution, once again, would probably be to mimic the mechanism of the gecko once more - if his hand is peeled away at an angle from the surface, the bonds are broken extraordinarily easily. The only problem that remains is how he would stick to walls through his costume - the van der Waals forces have an incredibly short range as previously discussed.
